In this article, I'll discuss how I extended my renderer to support triangles and simple color banding. This is the second article in my nodeTeapot series, where I continue exploring different approaches to 3D rendering. After rendering the Utah Teapot as a collection of points, the natural next step was to render it properly using triangles.

Goal

The goal of this version was twofold: First, to transition from rendering individual points to rendering filled triangles. Second, to experiment with simple color banding techniques to give the render a stylized appearance by discretizing colors in the style of old 8bit images.

Implementation

I started by implementing basic triangle rasterization. For each triangle, I computed its screen-space coordinates after projection, and filled it using a simple scanline algorithm. To handle depth properly, I used a z-buffer to ensure that nearer triangles obscure farther ones.

For color banding, I quantized the shading into a small number of discrete levels based on depth or the triangle's normal. This gives the result a somewhat artificial, posterized look — something between "retro 3D graphics" and "procedural art experiment." The brass color from the first version became optional; now I could experiment with any color palette I liked.

To demonstrate the improvements, I again rendered the Utah Teapot. However, I decided not to keep the other elements with it. This time, the teapot now appeared to be a solid object, not just collections of floating points. It almost looks like a normal 3D render.

Thoughts

This version of the renderer felt like a natural evolution from the point-cloud approach. It reinforced my understanding of projection, depth handling, and basic rasterization techniques. Additionally, experimenting with color banding gave me ideas for future procedural rendering styles.

Next, I plan to implement proper camera controls and object transformations. This will allow me to move objects independently and explore dynamic scenes, rather than relying on a static center-of-rotation.
